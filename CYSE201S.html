  • Week 6: Can we create three fake websites and three real websites and ask them to spot what makes them fake?

  • The average person might not be aware of the differences between a real website that they intend to use, and a malicious website that acts a front to gather information about an individual that intentionally appears legitimate. There are various differences in a fake website that should appear as red flags to a user. Firstly, the most common feature of a fake website is that the domain name may not appear correct. Even if a hacker has setup a website to look like an exact replica, the hacker cannot use the same domain name of the real one. Secondly, web browsers provide a symbol next to the URL that looks like a padlock or another related icon. Clicking on the icon allows you to view the digital certificate of the website, and almost all trustworthy sites will provide a legitimate certification to allow secure connections. If the padlock symbol has a warning sign on it, that may mean the digital certificate is not valid, and cannot be trusted. Lastly, if a user feels that they are not sure they have arrived at the correct website, either through a referral link from an email, or otherwise, the user can always manually go to the intended website themselves and perform the necessary activities from there. Users should look carefully at domain names, and ensure that they are spelled correctly, with no extra characters included to make the domain name appear legitimate. For example, a hacker looking to imitate ‘www.facebook.com’ may register the malicious address as ‘www.face.book.com’. If a URL does not appear to be spelled correctly, the user should not remain there. If a web browser warns you that a digital certificate could be not be validated, that may indicate that the website is unsafe, and users should take heed of said warnings.

  • Article 1: "Securing the Human: A Review of Literature on Broadening Diversity in Cybersecurity Education".

  • Securing the Human: A Review of Literature on Broadening Diversity in Cybersecurity Education demonstrates the opportunity to diversify the field of cybersecurity and the development of a diverse workforce of proficient computer scientists. This study includes interpretive data collection, through the method of observing and reviewing “literature concentrating on cybersecurity education initiatives for diversification”. There are three research questions present: “What is the current body of work concerning the diversification of the cybersecurity field”, “What are the gaps in education research for the diversification of the cybersecurity field”, and “What approaches are successful or unsuccessful in diversifying the cybersecurity field”. The study utilizes a variety of research methods, including “Open Literature Search Methodology”, “Focused Literature Search Methodology”, and “Coding Results”. The first phase, Open Literature Search Methodology, is defined in the study as “a broad review of literature based on standard approaches employed to increase participation in computing and cybersecurity”. Every member of the study’s working group formed a list of interventions to report on based on their experiences as educators. Each intervention was compared to evidence that had been studied heavily for its efficacy in Computer Science/STEM fields. The second phase, Focused Literature Search Methodology, involved a more specific group of keyword search terms in the ACM Digital Library, including terms like “cybersecurity diversity or under-representation or minorities”. All phases of the study used qualitative analysis to review the literature that was found. This study was effective at understanding cybersecurity through a social lens, as it highlighted the need for more guidelines for educators on how to foster a diverse educational community, in order to respond to the lack thereof. Overall, this study provided methods for educators to help entice and retain a diverse field of students who can join the cybersecurity field.
