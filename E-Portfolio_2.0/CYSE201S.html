  • Week 1: Review the NICE Framework. Are there certain areas that you would want to focus your career on? Explain which areas would appeal the most to you and which would appeal the least.

  • Focusing your skills in cybersecurity is incredibly important because the field is vast and expansive, and will only continue to keep expanding. Thus, it may be easier to hone your edge at a select few areas. The NICE framework lays out a beneficial guide to these areas and the jobs within them that need to be filled. There are three areas that I am most interested in: Analyze, Collect and Operate, and Investigate. First, within the Analyze area, exploitation/threat analysis seems very enticing me. Collecting data and studying the ways in which systems are vulnerable, or become vulnerable, seems like a great way to learn and gain experience in the industry. Second, within the Collect and Operate area, cyber operations is appealing to me because it feels very hands-on, and in my opinion that is one of the best ways to grow in anything. That is also where I feel a lot of work actually gets done, and the reward is very clear. Last, in the Investigate section, Digital Forensics has always enticed me ever since I first heard about it, due to the fact that you’re actively helping solve crime cases. The three areas that I wouldn’t explicitly look to join would be Operate and Maintain, Oversee and Govern, and Securely Provision. First, specifically in Operate and Maintain, customer service and tech support do not excite me at all due to the fact that I’m not a very good communicator in the moment, and that type of work requires you to be very stoic and clear-headed, and that doesn’t really fit my personality very well. Second, within Oversee and govern, training and educating people doesn’t speak to me because I do not view myself as the educator type. Last, within Securely Provision, testing and evaluating products or services seems to be a very mundane career, and I feel like I would get bored with that line of work very quickly, and become disinterested.

  • Week 2: Explain how the principles of science relate to cybersecurity.

  • The principles of science relate to cybersecurity in a variety of ways, including the scientific method, collecting data and analyzing it, and sharing newfound knowledge with the community. Cybersecurity experts use the scientific method every day, whether or not they realize it. Theorizing how a certain system or software is vulnerable, and testing that theory by performing a hack is very similar to drafting a hypothesis and then testing it. The collection of data and analyzing that data is also extremely important to the field of cybersecurity, including those who specialize in packet analysis or incident response. Lastly, sharing gained knowledge is extremely important as it is in the field of science because it helps to keep regular users in the loop, as well as educate experts on how to better protect their own systems.

  • Week 6: Can we create three fake websites and three real websites and ask them to spot what makes them fake?

  • The average person might not be aware of the differences between a real website that they intend to use, and a malicious website that acts a front to gather information about an individual that intentionally appears legitimate. There are various differences in a fake website that should appear as red flags to a user. Firstly, the most common feature of a fake website is that the domain name may not appear correct. Even if a hacker has setup a website to look like an exact replica, the hacker cannot use the same domain name of the real one. Secondly, web browsers provide a symbol next to the URL that looks like a padlock or another related icon. Clicking on the icon allows you to view the digital certificate of the website, and almost all trustworthy sites will provide a legitimate certification to allow secure connections. If the padlock symbol has a warning sign on it, that may mean the digital certificate is not valid, and cannot be trusted. Lastly, if a user feels that they are not sure they have arrived at the correct website, either through a referral link from an email, or otherwise, the user can always manually go to the intended website themselves and perform the necessary activities from there. Users should look carefully at domain names, and ensure that they are spelled correctly, with no extra characters included to make the domain name appear legitimate. For example, a hacker looking to imitate ‘www.facebook.com’ may register the malicious address as ‘www.face.book.com’. If a URL does not appear to be spelled correctly, the user should not remain there. If a web browser warns you that a digital certificate could be not be validated, that may indicate that the website is unsafe, and users should take heed of said warnings.
