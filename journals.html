Focusing your skills in cybersecurity is incredibly important because the field is vast and expansive, and will only continue to keep expanding. Thus, it may be easier to hone your edge at a select few areas. The NICE framework lays out a beneficial guide to these areas and the jobs within them that need to be filled. There are three areas that I am most interested in: Analyze, Collect and Operate, and Investigate. First, within the Analyze area, exploitation/threat analysis seems very enticing me. Collecting data and studying the ways in which systems are vulnerable, or become vulnerable, seems like a great way to learn and gain experience in the industry. Second, within the Collect and Operate area, cyber operations is appealing to me because it feels very hands-on, and in my opinion that is one of the best ways to grow in anything. That is also where I feel a lot of work actually gets done, and the reward is very clear. Last, in the Investigate section, Digital Forensics has always enticed me ever since I first heard about it, due to the fact that you’re actively helping solve crime cases. The three areas that I wouldn’t explicitly look to join would be Operate and Maintain, Oversee and Govern, and Securely Provision. First, specifically in Operate and Maintain, customer service and tech support do not excite me at all due to the fact that I’m not a very good communicator in the moment, and that type of work requires you to be very stoic and clear-headed, and that doesn’t really fit my personality very well. Second, within Oversee and govern, training and educating people doesn’t speak to me because I do not view myself as the educator type. Last, within Securely Provision, testing and evaluating products or services seems to be a very mundane career, and I feel like I would get bored with that line of work very quickly, and become disinterested.

The principles of science relate to cybersecurity in a variety of ways, including the scientific method, collecting data and analyzing it, and sharing newfound knowledge with the community. Cybersecurity experts use the scientific method every day, whether or not they realize it. Theorizing how a certain system or software is vulnerable, and testing that theory by performing a hack is very similar to drafting a hypothesis and then testing it. The collection of data and analyzing that data is also extremely important to the field of cybersecurity, including those who specialize in packet analysis or incident response. Lastly, sharing gained knowledge is extremely important as it is in the field of science because it helps to keep regular users in the loop, as well as educate experts on how to better protect their own systems.

Researchers might use the information to study data breaches by looking at the common themes among them. If patterns are to be found, they can prove extremely useful in breaking the cycle and preventing more breaches in the future. Common vulnerabilities like stolen passwords, outdated software, or social engineering are all common patterns that a researcher might be able to prevent in the future.

There are a multitude of digital experiences that have filled my needs as it relates to Maslow’s hierarchy of needs. For self-actualization, I’ve used digital technology as a creative outlet, as some of my hobbies now include 3D modeling and game development. Although, I don’t think I’ve realized my “full potential” in those outlets, but I feel that I still have plenty of time left to do so. For my esteem needs, I usually enjoy video games that allow you to progress as you play, and that progression gives me a feeling of accomplishment. Whether or not that’s considered “worthwhile”, that’s up to the individual in my opinion. For belonging needs, I use social media to talk to my friends regularly, something that I don’t think I could have done without it, as most of my friends live pretty far away from me. For safety needs, the fact that I can get alerts straight to my phone about bad weather is extremely beneficial, as well as the alarms in my house are all automated, so I don’t have to worry about not knowing if there is someone breaking into my house is also very nice. For physiological needs, I can order food and water with a click of a button from an app, and can also use apps to track how well I slept during the night, which meets those needs very well.

I rank the “political” motive number one because it makes the most sense out of all the motives, due to the fact that politics is extremely controversial by nature and such an area can turn anyone into an activist about anything if they dive in far enough. I rank the “for money” motive a close second, because money is an extremely valuable resource to literally everyone, and it does not come as a surprise at all that people may commit cybercrime for financial gain. I rank the “revenge” motive as third because those who might feel they’ve been egregiously wronged in some way might make drastic decisions as a result of that feeling. I rank the “recognition” motive fourth due to the fact that its very easy for the act of finding vulnerabilities to become a competition of sorts, and if you’re the first person to find a vulnerability, it’s a good demonstration of your hacking abilities for all to see. I rank the “multiple reasons” motive fifth, because while it’s definitely understandable that someone’s view of the world influences their decisions, there seems to be a stronger motive under that layer, like financial gain and political activism. I rank the “entertainment” motive sixth because while a lot of people partake in activities just for fun, another aspect of said activity allows people to willingly spend more time on it that is more enticing. An example from the article is that even thought he perpetrator said he did it for fun, he ended up auctioning off the data for money anyways. I rank the “boredom” motive last because from what I read in the article it didn’t really seem like a motive in the first place versus something that gets individuals caught in a trap. Even then, it seems like a little weak because boredom is just a symptom of an underlying problem that causes you to take action.
